2. The Statistical Description of Physical Systems

2.1 Microstates and Macrostates

  • Assumption: The system can exist in a discrete (but possibly infinite) set of microstates. A microstate defines the values of all possible microscopic variables.
  • For example, in a classical system of point particles, for example, a microstate defines the position and momentum of every particle. In a quantum mechanical system, it defines th. e value of the wavefunction at every point in space.
  • we assume microstates are discrete
  • A macrostate is defined by specifying the value of every macroscopic variable.

2.2 The Density of States

  • \(\Omega(E,V,\dots)\) the number of microstates corresponding to a macrostate.
  • \(\logΩ(E)\propto E\)

2.3 The Postulate of Equal a priori Probabilities

  • Assumption: A system has an equal probability of being in any microstate that is consistent with its current macrostate.
  • Definition: An isolated system that satisfies the postulate of equal a priori probabilities is said to be in equilibrium.

2.4 Time Averages and Ensemble Averages

  • *Definition: A system for which time averages and ensemble averages are equal is said to be ergodic.
  • Definition: An isolated system is in equilibrium if the probability distribution of its microstates does not change with time.

2.5 The Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

  • Isolated system: \(A\) and \(B\)
    • energy of system \(E_T=E_A+E_B+E_{AB}\)
    • Assumption: \(E_{AB}\) is small enough that we can ignore it
    • \(E_T \approx E_A + E_B\) (weakly coupled)
    • Assumption: The degrees of freedom of A and B are specified independently so the density of states factorizes: \(\Omega_T=\Omega_A\Omega_B\)
    • Assumption: The log of the density of states of B can be approximated as linear in energy: \(\log(\Omega_B(E_B))\approx \alpha + \beta E_B\) (Any function can be approximated as linear over sufficiently small intervals; B must be much larger than A)
    • Question: if the total energy of the system is ET, what is the probability of A being in one particular microstate whose energy is EA?
      • The probability of A being in the desired microstate equals the fraction of microstates of the whole system for which A is in that microstate.
      • \(p(E_A)\propto \Omega_B(E_T-E_A)\propto e^{-\beta E_A}\)
      • define \(\dfrac{1}{kT}\equiv \beta \equiv \dfrac{\partial \log(\Omega_B(E))}{\partial E}\)
      • \(p(E_A)\propto e^{-E_A/kT}\)
      • partition function \(Z=\sum e^{-E_A/kT}\)
      • Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution \(p(E_A)=\dfrac{e^{-E_A/kT}}{Z}\)

2.6 Thermodynamic Forces

  • other macroscopic variable
  • volume (\(V\))
    • \(\log(\Omega_B(E_B,V_B))\approx \alpha + \beta E_B + \gamma V_b\)
    • \(p(E_A,V_A)\propto \Omega_B(E_T-E_A,V_T-V_A)\propto e^{-\beta E_A - \gamma V_A}\)
    • define \(P\equiv kT \dfrac{\partial \log(\Omega_B(E,V))}{\partial V}\)
    • \(p(E_A,V_A)=\dfrac{e^{-(E_A+PV_A)/kT}}{Z}\) with \(Z=\sum e^{-(E_A+PV_A)/kT}\)
  • number of particles (\(N\))
    • define \(\mu\equiv -kT \dfrac{\partial \log(\Omega_B(E,N))}{\partial N}\)
    • \(p(E_A,N_A)=\dfrac{e^{-(E_A-\mu N_A)/kT}}{Z}\) with \(Z=\sum e^{-(E_A-\mu N_A)/kT}\)
  • thermodynamic forces
    • \(P\) \(T\) \(\mu\)
    • conjugate to the macroscopic variable

2.7 Probabilites of Macrostates

  • \(p(E_A)=\sum\dfrac{e^{-E_A/kT}}{Z}=\dfrac{\Omega_A(E_A) e^{-E_A/kT}}{Z}\)
  • define \(S=k\cdot \log(\Omega_A)\)
  • \(p(E_A)=\dfrac{e^{-(E_A-TS)/kT}}{Z}\)

2.8 Thermodynamic Potentials

  • general form \(p=\dfrac{e^{-\Phi/kT}}{Z}\)
  • thermodynamic potentials \(\Phi\)
    • \(H=E+PV\) (Enthalpy)
    • \(A=E-TS\) (Helmholtz free energy)
    • \(G=E+PV-TS\) (Gibbs free energy)
    • \(\Phi_G=E-\mu N-TS\) (Grand potential)
  • ensembles
    • microcanonical ensemble (\(NVE\))
    • canonical ensemble (\(NVT\))
    • grand canonical ensemble (\(\mu VT\))
    • isothermal–isobaric ensemble (\(NPT\))

2.9 Averages

  • ensemble average \(\langle x \rangle = \dfrac{\sum_i x_i e^{-\Phi_i/kT}}{\sum_i e^{-\Phi_i/kT}}\)
  • variance \(Var(x)=\langle (x-\langle x \rangle)^2 \rangle = \langle x^2 \rangle - \langle x \rangle^2\)
  • standard deviation \(\sigma=\sqrt{Var(x)}\)
  • derivatives of \(\log(Z)\) tend to give averages
    • \(-\dfrac{\partial \log(Z)}{\partial \beta} = \langle \Phi \rangle\)
    • \(-kT \dfrac{\partial \log(Z)}{\partial x} = \langle \dfrac{\partial \Phi}{\partial x} \rangle\)

4. The Interpretation of Statistical Quantities

4.1 Temerature

  • equipartition theorem
    • the energy of a system as a function of its microscopic variables \(E(x_1,x_2,\dots)\)
      • it can be separated into two terms \(E=E_1(x_1)+E_2(x_2,x_3,\dots)\)
      • \(x_1\) can be treated as a countinuous variable \(x_1\in [-\infty,\infty]\)
      • the energy is quadratic in \(x_1\) \(E_1(x_1)=Cx_1^2\)
    • consider the kinetic energy as a function of momentum variable \(p_i\): \(E_i=p_i^2/2m\)
    • \(\langle E_1 \rangle=\dfrac{kT}{2}\)
    • Interpretation of Temperature: If a system is in equilibrium with a heat bath at temperature \(T\), then its average kinetic energy is \(kT/2\) per degree of freedom.

4.2 Thermodynamic Potentials as a Measure of Probability

  • Thermodynamic potentials mix together quantities realted to two different systems.
    • for example \(G_A=E_A+P_BV_A-T_BS_A\)
      • \(G_A\) how probable it is for a system to be in a particular state
      • lower \(E_A\) are more likely to occur if all else being unchanged
      • lower \(V_A\) are more likely to occur if all else being unchanged
      • \(S_A\) are more likely to increase to lower \(G_A\), which corrosponds to increasing \(E_A\) and \(V_A\)

4.3 Independent and Dependent Variables

  • If each macrostate has a well defined value for each macroscopic variable and consists of a fixed set of microstates, then the thermodynamic potential also has a well defined value. It describes the probability for the system to be in that macrostate.
  • If we instead treat each macrostate as a probability distribution over microstates, then the thermodynamic potential no longer has a strictly fixed value. Instead we must view it as an average quantity.

4.4 Thermodynamic Potentials and Thermodynamic Forces

  • a generalized force \(Q=-\dfrac{\partial U}{\partial x}\)
  • Mechnical Equilibrium
    • \(\dfrac{\partial G_A}{\partial V_A}=0=\dfrac{\partial E_A}{\partial V_A}+P_B-P_A\)
    • \(-\dfrac{\partial E_A}{\partial V_A}=P_B-P_A\)
  • Chemical potential Equilibrium
    • \(\dfrac{\partial \Phi_{GA}}{\partial N_A}=0=\dfrac{\partial E_A}{\partial N_A}-\mu_B+\mu_A\)
    • \(-\dfrac{\partial E_A}{\partial N_A}=\mu_A-\mu_B\)
